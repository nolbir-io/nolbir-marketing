## Slide 2 — Marketplaces start your business. They don't run it.

**Headline (on-slide):**

> Marketplaces start your business. They don't run it.

**Body (3 short bullets, on-slide):**

- Fixed take rate. Compressing margin.
- Rented customers. No second-sale economics.
- Channel concentration. Single-vendor risk.

**Visual direction:**

- Single image: a stylized marketplace listing tile (one row in a long list) with the merchant's brand name visible. Subtle pixelation/distance effect on everything around it — to communicate "you're a row in someone else's list."
- No charts. No stock photography.

**Speaker notes / talk track:**

> "You've already solved the hardest part — distribution. The orders come in. So why are we having this conversation?
>
> Because every operator on Uzum or Yandex Market eventually hits the same three walls. Take rate is the same on every order, but your costs of goods, ops, and acquisition all move against you as you scale — so profit doesn't scale with revenue. The marketplace owns the customer relationship and the data, so the second sale costs you the same as the first. And one fee bump or ranking tweak can move your revenue by double digits — and you don't get a vote.
>
> The question isn't whether to leave marketplaces. It's whether marketplaces should be your *only* channel."

**Why this slide (do not delete):** This is the buyer's actual world. If you skip the problem and jump to product, the rest of the deck has no foundation.

- If the founders have an actual customer who **closed a physical store** or **chose online over a planned store**, replace the closing line with their quote and number. Concrete > theoretical.

**Why this slide:** Reinforces the cost-of-status-quo argument with the most defensible numbers in the deck — concrete rent, renovation, utilities, and staffing figures from the founders' direct knowledge of the Tashkent retail market. Goes beyond cost: physical retail is also bound by **operating hours, per-transaction staffing, and geographic catchment**. Marketplaces solve the hours problem but not the take rate or customer-ownership problem. Direct online channels are the only path that scales 24/7, self-serve, and without per-store overhead. Particularly powerful for merchants who already operate physical retail, are weighing retail expansion against online investment, or have an economic buyer (CFO/owner) in the room. Pairs with Slide 3 to complete the cost case: marketplaces drain margin, physical retail drains capital and caps reach — direct online channels avoid both.

## Appendix A4 — Commission comparison & payback math
