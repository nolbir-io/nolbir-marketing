# Founder & Team Alignment on the Company Thesis

> **Purpose of this doc.** Force the founding team to write down — in the same words — what we believe, why we're the ones to build it, and what would make us change our minds. If two founders read this back-to-back and disagree on a sentence, we've found something worth a conversation today (cheap) instead of a year from now (expensive).

> **How to use it.** Each founder fills in their own answers independently first. Then sit together and reconcile. Save the reconciled version. Re-read it every quarter. Update with a changelog — never overwrite silently.

## 1. The Thesis in One Sentence

Write a single sentence that a smart friend who doesn't work in your space could repeat back without help. If it needs a comma and a clause, it's too long.

**Template:**
> We believe that **[shift / unmet need]** is creating a **[window / opportunity]**, and the company that wins it will be the one that **[unique approach]**.

**Your version:**
> We believe that e-commerce is becoming mature, and the company that wins it will be full-stack operating system, where merchants rely it on from launch to transaction.

**Stress test (answer in one line each):**
- Could a competitor write the exact same sentence? If yes, sharpen it.
- Does it imply what we will *not* do?
- Is the belief actually contrarian, or is it consensus dressed up?

**Reference points (how others have framed it):**
- *Airbnb (early):* People will pay to stay in strangers' homes if trust is engineered into the product.
- *Stripe:* Accepting payments online should be a few lines of code, not a six-week integration.
- *Figma:* Design tools belong in the browser, multiplayer by default.
- *Notion:* Software should be Lego blocks the user assembles, not finished products they conform to.
- *Anduril:* Defense tech should be built like consumer software — software-first, capital-efficient, sold commercially off-the-shelf.

## 2. Why Now

A great thesis is wrong for ten years and then right for eighteen months. Name what changed.

**Fill in the three forces:**

**a) Technology shift** — With AI it is become sustainable to build custom storefronts, make integrations faster
>

**b) Market / regulatory shift** — Big marketplaces are teaching how to buy online
>

**c) Cultural / behavioral shift** — They are willing to insert their card and pay online
>

**The "why not 5 years ago" test:**
> Five years ago, this company would have failed because: Building such platform took ages

**The "why not 5 years from now" test:**
> If we wait five years, the window closes because: We will have many players and it become friction to move to different platform

**Reference points:**
- *Uber:* Smartphones with GPS reached mass penetration; trust in cashless payments crossed a threshold.
- *Doordash (YC S13):* Restaurants now had POS systems and smartphones; customers expected on-demand.
- *OpenAI / foundation-model-era startups:* Inference cost per token dropped ~10x in 18 months; latency made interactive UX possible.

## 3. What We Believe That Most People Don't

a16z and YC partners both push hard on this question. The point isn't to be edgy — it's to surface where your conviction differs from the room. If everyone you pitch nods along, you're probably not telling them anything.

**Template:**
> Most people in our space believe **[consensus view]**.
> We believe **[contrarian view]**.
> We believe this because **[evidence — usually something we've seen up close]**.
> We will be proven right when **[observable signal]**.

**Your version:**
> It is becoming increasingly complex to manage your every channel
> We believe, from single dashboard you could manage your sales channels
> Every actor will have their personalized view, accountant, CFO, category managers, marketing specials, owners, investors

**Reference points:**
- *Airbnb's contrarian belief:* Hosts would let strangers into their homes (every investor said no).
- *Stripe's contrarian belief:* Developers, not CFOs, were the buyers of payments infrastructure.
- *Figma's contrarian belief:* Designers would tolerate browser-based tools that were initially worse than Sketch, in exchange for collaboration.
- *Coinbase's contrarian belief (2012):* Regular consumers — not just crypto natives — would buy Bitcoin if the UX looked like a bank app.

## Appendix A: How a16z and YC partners actually evaluate this

Distilled from public talks, essays, and partner letters — paraphrased, not quoted at length.

**a16z (Marc Andreessen, Ben Horowitz, partner essays):**
- "Strong opinions, strongly held." Vague theses lose. Specific, falsifiable theses win.
- Founder-market fit > market size in seed conversations. Why *you* is the first filter.
- The best founders have a "secret" — a non-consensus belief earned from lived experience, not from reading the same blog posts everyone else read.
- Look for narrative violation: the founder is doing something that sounds dumb to most smart people, for a reason that becomes obvious in hindsight.

**YC (Paul Graham, Michael Seibel, Dalton Caldwell):**
- "What do you know that others don't?" — the single most-asked question in the YC interview.
- "Make something people want" presupposes you know who *people* are. The thesis names the user, the pain, and the wedge.
- Founders should be able to explain the company in two sentences to their parents. If they can't, they don't understand it themselves yet.
- "Default alive vs. default dead" thinking applies to the thesis too: is this thesis default-true (consensus, crowded) or default-false (contrarian, lonely but with upside)?

**Common patterns across both:**
1. The thesis is written down and revisited — it's not a vibe.
2. Founder-market fit is specific and personal, not generic ("we love this space").
3. The team can name what would prove them wrong.
4. Disagreements between cofounders are surfaced early, in writing.
5. The "why now" is sharp enough that a delay of 12 months would meaningfully hurt.

## Appendix B: Reference deck patterns (one slide / one paragraph each)

**Pattern: Airbnb (the now-famous 2008 deck)** — Team slide was three faces, one line each, role-focused. The thesis lived in the "Problem" and "Market Validation" slides, not the team slide.

**Pattern: Sequoia memo template** — Asks for "Why now" as a standalone section, separate from market and product. Treat them as independent claims.

**Pattern: First Round "Founder/Market Fit" essay (Mike Maples-style)** — Each founder writes a paragraph on the lived experience that led them to this problem. Specific moments, not categories.

**Pattern: Bezos two-page memo** — Force prose, ban bullets in v1. Bullets let teams hide disagreement; prose forces it out.
